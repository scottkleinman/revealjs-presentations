Saintly Sidekicks in the South English Legendary

International Medieval Congress, Kalamazoo, May 12, 2017

Scott Kleinman
California State University, Northridge
scott.kleinman@csun.edu

1. The Life of St Ursula, ll. 103-106

Þat child þat scholde þis mayde wedde, ase heo hadde i-seid,
Kyng was i-maud of Enguelonde þo is fader was ded.
Þat ladde swiþe chaste lijf and longuede swiþe sore
With þat clene maide to speke and beo þoruȝ strencþe of Godes lore.

That child who would wed the maid, as she had said
Was made King of England when his father was dead.
He led a very chaste life and longed very sorely
To speak and be with that pure paid.

2. The Life of St Ursula, ll. 118-119

Mest ioye heo made with is ȝongue soster, þat hiet Florentyne,
Þat heo, clene mayde, scholde soffrie deþes pine.

Most joy she [Ursula] made with his young sister, who was called Florentine,
That she, a pure maid, should suffer death’s pain.

3. The Life of St Ursula, ll. 121-126

Ourse of Brutayne þene ȝoungue king in grete ioye of loue gan lere
Þat he him heolde faste to hire and ne bilefde for no fere,
Þat huy miȝhten in trewe loue to-gadere i-martred beo
And wonye in heuene with Ihesu Crist, þat bouȝte heom on þe treo.
Al þis grauntede þis ȝoungue king, and is lefmane nouȝt ne wernde;
His furste þouȝt into treowe loue and into clannesse he turnde.

Ursula of Brittany in the great joy of love began to teach the young king
So that she held him fast to he and did not let go from any fear,
So that they might in true love be martyred together
And dwell in heaven with Jesus Christ, who redeemed them on the tree.
This young king granted all this, and refused his beloved nothing;
His first thought he turned into true love and into purity.

4. The Life of St Ursula, ll. 138-142

And þus heo and al hire compaygnie to þe ioye of heuene gan wiende—
And þe ȝongue king of Enguelonde, and is moder also,
And is ȝongue soster Florentine, to deþe weren i-do,
And þe pope Ciriac and bischopes mani on
Þat for trewe hope of martyrdom with heom weren i-gon.

And thus she and all her company went to the joy of heaven—
And the young king of England, and his mother also,
And his young sister Florentine, were all put to death,
As well as the pop Cyriacus and many a bishop
Who for true desire for martyrdom had gone with them.

5. The Life of St Mary of Egypt, ll. 161-162

Ne wilne þou nouȝt þat Ich schewie þe, oþur wummen forto schende,
Mi licame, for Ich naked am; ake lat me awei fram þe wende.

You do not want me to show you my body, to shame other women,
For I am naked; but let me go away from you.

6. The Life of St Mary of Egypt, ll. 170-174

“Graunte me þat Ich mote in þine beden beo.”
Þis monek ful adoun akne, and heo aȝein him also,
And eyþer bad oþures blessingue, and noþur nolde it do.
Ase heo leien longue togadere, bitwene heom oþur word huy ne sede
Bote “Blesse þu me” ake “blesse þou me”; þe monek swatte for drede.

“Grant me that I may be in your prayers.”
This monk fell down on his knees, and she before him also,
And each asked for the other’s blessing, and neither would do it.
As they lay long together, they said no other words between them
Save “May you bless me”, no, “May you bless me”. The monk sweated for fear.

7. The Life of St Mary of Egypt, ll. 179-180

Hou is þe stat of holi churche i-loked, and Christinedom?
And hou is it of þe heþene prince, þat heore riȝtes heom binam?

How is the state of holy church looked after, and Christendom?
And how is it by the heathen prince, who took their rights from them?
